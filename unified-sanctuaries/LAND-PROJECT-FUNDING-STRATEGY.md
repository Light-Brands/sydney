## The Capital Stack: How Different Money Sources Layer Together

The most successful land-based community projects don't rely on one type of funding. They build a **capital stack** -- layers of different capital types, each suited to a different purpose, each unlocking the next.

```
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|                    EARNED REVENUE                      |
|        (sustains operations once on the land)          |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|              COMMUNITY CAPITAL                         |
|    (members, residents, aligned individuals)           |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|              GRANTS & PHILANTHROPY                     |
|   (foundations, government, donor-advised funds)       |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|              LOANS & PATIENT CAPITAL                   |
|  (CDFIs, USDA, mission-aligned lenders, investors)    |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|              ANCHOR INVESTMENT                         |
|    (lead investor, land donor, or major funder)        |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
```

Each layer serves a distinct function. Here's how to maximize each one.

## Layer 1: Anchor Investment -- The Lead Capital

**What it is:** A single large commitment (or 2-3 major commitments) that provides the foundation for acquisition and signals credibility to every other funder.

**Your current approach:** Seeking an aligned investor-partner to acquire a campus and place it into a Community Land Trust.

**How to maximize it:**

- **Frame the ask as a legacy investment, not a donation.** Your investor business plan already does this well. The CLT structure means the land is permanently stewarded -- this is a legacy vehicle. Emphasize that the investor's capital creates permanent infrastructure, not a one-time program.

- **Offer structured terms.** Even within a CLT model, an anchor investor can receive:
  - A revenue-sharing arrangement from the four pillars (capped return, e.g., 2-5% annually until principal is returned)
  - Naming rights or stewardship recognition
  - A seat on the Land Trust board (advisory, not controlling)
  - First-look access to invest in subsequent sanctuary sites

- **Use the anchor commitment to unlock everything else.** A signed LOI or term sheet from a lead investor is the single most powerful tool for unlocking grants ("we have committed acquisition capital"), loans ("we have equity in the deal"), and community investment ("a major investor has already committed -- join us").

- **Don't wait for the full amount.** A $2-3M anchor commitment is enough to begin acquisition conversations, especially for distressed campus properties where sellers are motivated.

## Layer 2: Loans & Patient Capital -- Leverage Without Giving Up the Mission

**What it is:** Borrowed money with favorable terms, used specifically for acquisition and infrastructure -- not operations.

**Sources to pursue aggressively:**

### USDA Programs (Vermont is highly eligible)

| Program | What It Funds | Typical Amount | Key Details |
|---------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| **USDA Community Facilities Direct Loan** | Acquisition and renovation of community facilities | $100K - $10M+ | Low interest (currently ~3.5-4.5%), up to 40-year terms. Serves rural communities under 20,000 population. A campus conversion fits perfectly. |
| **USDA Community Facilities Grant** | Same as above, but grant (not loan) | Up to $500K | Can be stacked on top of the loan. Prioritizes communities under 5,500 population -- Guilford and Putney qualify. |
| **USDA Beginning Farmer & Rancher** | Farm infrastructure, equipment, land | Up to $600K (direct) | For the farm pillar specifically. You have team members who qualify as beginning farmers. |
| **USDA Rural Business Development Grant** | Technical assistance, training, planning | $10K - $500K | Can fund the planning and feasibility work for the campus conversion. |
| **USDA Rural Energy for America (REAP)** | Renewable energy, energy efficiency | Grants up to $1M, loans up to $25M | For solar, geothermal, energy retrofits on campus buildings. |

**Action:** Contact the USDA Rural Development Vermont State Office directly. They have dedicated staff who help applicants navigate which programs to combine. Vermont is a priority state for rural development funding.

### Vermont State Programs

| Program | What It Funds | Key Details |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Vermont Housing & Conservation Board (VHCB)** | Affordable housing, farmland conservation, community facilities | VHCB is one of the most progressive state housing/conservation agencies in the country. They fund CLTs directly and have experience with multi-use community projects. A campus-to-village conversion aligns with their mission. |
| **Vermont Community Development Program (VCDP)** | Community facilities, housing, economic development | Federal CDBG money administered by the state. Can fund infrastructure improvements on the campus. |
| **Vermont Community Foundation** | Community projects across Vermont | They offer both grants and program-related investments. Strong interest in innovative community models. |
| **Vermont Farm & Forest Viability Program** | Business planning for farm operations | Free business planning assistance for the farm pillar. |

### Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)

CDFIs are mission-driven lenders that provide capital to projects traditional banks won't touch. They are specifically designed for community-oriented projects like yours.

| CDFI | Focus | Why It Fits |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Vermont Community Loan Fund (VCLF)** | Affordable housing, community facilities, small business in Vermont | They have directly funded CLT projects and cooperative housing. They understand Vermont's landscape. |
| **Cooperative Fund of New England (CFNE)** | Cooperatives, community-owned enterprises | Perfect for the cooperative living pillar and any co-op businesses within the village. Loans from $10K to $1M+. |
| **RSF Social Finance** | Regenerative enterprises, social enterprises | They already operate in the regenerative economy space. Loans from $200K to $10M. They also offer a Donor Advised Fund that could be a pipeline for aligned donors. |
| **Boston Community Capital / LISC** | Community development, affordable housing | Larger-scale financing for the housing components. |
| **Natural Capital Investment Fund** | Natural resource-based enterprises, sustainable agriculture | Could fund the farm and retreat components specifically. |

### Other Patient Capital Sources

- **Program-Related Investments (PRIs) from foundations:** Some foundations (Ford, MacArthur, Kresge, Heron) make below-market loans from their endowments to mission-aligned projects. A CLT campus conversion is exactly the kind of project PRIs are designed for. The Novo Foundation, which already funds Kinship Earth, also makes PRIs.

- **New Markets Tax Credits (NMTC):** If the campus is in a qualified census tract (parts of Southern Vermont qualify), NMTC can provide a 39% federal tax credit over 7 years to investors, effectively reducing the cost of capital by nearly 40%. This is complex but powerful for a $5-10M acquisition.

- **Historic Tax Credits:** If the campus has buildings on or eligible for the National Register of Historic Places, federal and state historic tax credits can cover 20-45% of renovation costs.

**How to maximize loans:**

1. **Stack them.** A single acquisition can combine a USDA Community Facilities loan for the overall campus, a USDA Beginning Farmer loan for the agricultural land, a VCLF loan for the housing component, and VHCB funding for conservation -- all on the same property.

2. **Use grants to reduce the loan amount.** Every grant dollar reduces how much you need to borrow, which reduces debt service, which makes the revenue projections more achievable.

3. **Match loan terms to revenue timelines.** Seek interest-only periods for the first 12-24 months while revenue ramps up. USDA and CDFI lenders commonly offer this.

4. **Use the CLT structure as an asset.** Lenders serving community development missions (CDFIs, USDA) actively prefer lending to CLTs because the land is permanently affordable -- it reduces their risk of mission drift.

## Layer 4: Community Capital -- Your People Are Your Strongest Asset

**What it is:** Financial participation from community members who want to be part of this -- not as charity, but as stakeholders.

This is where your 2,200+ network becomes a funding engine. Here are the structures that work:

### Community Investment Models

**1. Community Investment Notes (through a CDFI or fiscal sponsor)**

- Community members invest $1,000-$50,000 in notes that pay a modest return (1-3%) over 5-10 years
- Capital is used for acquisition or infrastructure
- RSF Social Finance and Cooperative Fund of New England both facilitate these
- **Example:** RSF's Community Investment Notes allow individuals to invest as little as $1,000 at 0-1.5% return, with capital deployed to regenerative projects
- **Your advantage:** You already have a warm community. 100 people investing $5,000 each = $500K

**2. Cooperative Membership / Buy-In**

- Future residents and business operators purchase a membership share
- Shares range from $5,000-$50,000 depending on the level of access and commitment
- Shares are refundable upon exit (this is how cooperative housing works)
- **Structure:** The CLT holds the land; a cooperative entity manages operations. Membership shares capitalize the cooperative, not the land trust.
- **Your advantage:** You already describe Trust Units that are "liquidatable on exit" -- this maps directly to cooperative membership shares

**3. Revenue-Participation Agreements**

- Community investors contribute capital and receive a share of revenue from a specific pillar
- Example: 10 people each invest $10,000 into the Event Venue pillar and receive a pro-rata share of 5% of event revenue until they've received 1.2x their investment
- This is not equity -- it's a contractual revenue share
- **Why it works:** It gives investors a concrete, understandable return tied to real business activity

**4. Crowdfunding Campaigns**

- Use platforms like **Wefunder** (for investment crowdfunding under Reg CF -- up to $5M) or **StartEngine**
- Or use donation-based platforms like **GoFundMe** or **GiveSendGo** for specific milestones
- **Reg CF investment crowdfunding** allows anyone to invest as little as $100 in exchange for revenue shares, debt, or other securities -- no accredited investor requirement
- **Your advantage:** Your ecosystem has built-in media capacity (Live with Syd, Micelio Media) to drive campaign visibility

**5. Sweat Equity / Labor Contributions**

- Community members contribute labor in exchange for Trust Units, Voice Tokens, or reduced housing fees
- This is standard practice in cooperative housing and ecovillage development
- **Quantify it:** Track volunteer hours at a fair market rate ($25-35/hr). This becomes match funding for grants (many grants require matching, and in-kind labor counts)
- **Your advantage:** Permatours already mobilizes volunteer labor at scale. 50 volunteers x 40 hours = 2,000 hours = $50-70K in match value per event season

### How to Structure Community Capital Legally

Work with **Mycelial Law** (already on your team) and a securities attorney to:

1. **Choose the right exemption.** Reg CF (up to $5M from anyone), Reg D 506(b) (unlimited from accredited investors + up to 35 non-accredited), or Reg A+ (up to $75M with SEC qualification).

2. **Create offering documents.** Even for community-scale investment, you need an offering circular, subscription agreements, and risk disclosures. This protects both you and your investors.

3. **Use a cooperative structure.** If you form a cooperative (e.g., a Limited Cooperative Association under Vermont law), member shares are often exempt from federal securities registration, dramatically simplifying compliance.

4. **Vermont has favorable cooperative law.** Vermont's Limited Cooperative Association Act is among the most flexible in the country. It allows for both patron members (residents, workers) and investor members (community investors), with protections ensuring patron members retain governance control.

## Layer 5: Earned Revenue -- Sustaining Everything Once You're On the Land

Your four-pillar model ($1.4M-$4.5M/year at maturity) is well-designed for revenue diversity. To maximize it as a funding tool **before** you're fully operational:

### Pre-Acquisition Revenue

1. **Sell memberships now.** Offer "Founding Member" status at $50-500/year. Benefits include governance participation, priority housing application, discounted retreat/event rates, and community access. Even at modest scale, this proves market demand to lenders and investors.

2. **Host events on partner sites.** Permatours already does this. Brand some events as "Unified Sanctuaries presents..." to build the brand and generate revenue before you have your own land.

3. **Pre-sell retreat packages.** Offer "Founding Retreat" packages at a discount for the first year of operation. This generates cash now and creates committed customers.

4. **Offer consulting and design services.** Your team has deep expertise in permaculture design, natural building, cooperative governance, and regenerative finance. Sell that expertise to other land projects. This generates revenue AND builds your reputation.

### Post-Acquisition Revenue Maximization

Focus the first 90 days on the **highest-margin, lowest-setup-cost** revenue streams:

1. **Event venue rental** -- campus spaces are ready-made. Weddings and corporate retreats have the highest per-event revenue.
2. **Workshop programming** -- low overhead, high margin, builds community pipeline.
3. **Farm stand / cafe** -- if there's existing agricultural infrastructure, this can launch fast.
4. **Campground** -- minimal infrastructure needed, generates immediate revenue from overnight stays.

## Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| Can't find the right property | Expand search beyond university campuses to large rural properties with existing infrastructure (summer camps, conference centers, retreat centers closing) |
| Anchor investor doesn't materialize | Shift to a CDFI-led acquisition with USDA financing as the primary capital; smaller initial property; longer timeline |
| Grants don't come through at projected levels | Grants are the most uncertain layer -- never plan operations around them. Use grants for programming and improvements, not debt service |
| Community investment is slower than expected | Start small, prove the model, expand. 50 investors at $5K is more achievable than 5 at $50K |
| Revenue takes longer to ramp | Negotiate interest-only periods on all loans. Keep a 12-month operating reserve before acquisition closes |
| Regulatory / zoning issues | Engage Vermont planning and zoning early. CLTs have specific protections under Vermont law. VHCB can advocate on your behalf |

## Summary

You have more capital sources available than you're currently activating. The strategy is:

1. **Stack, don't choose.** Combine grants + loans + community capital + anchor investment + earned revenue. No single source needs to carry the full weight.

2. **Sequence for leverage.** Each commitment unlocks the next. Anchor investment unlocks loans. Loans + anchor unlock grants. All of the above unlock community investment.

3. **Activate your community as investors, not just donors.** 2,200+ people is a funding base. Give them structured ways to participate financially -- cooperative shares, community notes, monthly giving, sweat equity.

4. **Use Vermont's infrastructure.** USDA, VHCB, VCLF, Vermont cooperative law -- Vermont is one of the most favorable states in the country for exactly this kind of project.

5. **Start before you have the land.** Memberships, monthly giving, grant applications, USDA conversations, community investment offering documents -- all of this can happen now.

The land is the catalyst, but the community is the capital.
