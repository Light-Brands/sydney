# Moving to Mexico as a Kinship Earth Employee

**What to Consider: Taxes, Visas, Employment Law, Land, Banking, and Foundation Compliance**

*Working Document | March 2026*

---

## Overview

This document covers what you need to think through -- personally and organizationally -- if you're living in Mexico while employed by Kinship Earth, a US-based 501(c)(3) private foundation. It's organized by topic area, with action items at the end.

## 1. Employment Structure

### The Problem

When a US employer has a W-2 employee living and working in another country, the employer may have legal obligations in that country. Mexico's Federal Labor Law (Ley Federal del Trabajo) can apply to anyone physically performing work on Mexican soil -- regardless of where the employer is based.

If Mexican labor law applies, the employer is required to provide:

- **Aguinaldo:** Christmas bonus equal to at least 15 days' wages (paid by December 20)
- **Prima vacacional:** Vacation premium of 25% of vacation pay
- **PTU (Participacion de los Trabajadores en las Utilidades):** Profit sharing -- 10% of pre-tax profits distributed to employees. Nonprofits may be exempt, but this needs to be confirmed.
- **IMSS enrollment:** Registration with Mexico's social security system (covers healthcare, disability, retirement). Employer contributions run roughly 25-30% on top of salary.
- **Infonavit:** Housing fund contributions (~5% of salary)
- **Severance protections:** Mexico's labor law heavily favors employees. Termination without cause requires 3 months' salary plus 20 days per year of service, plus prorated aguinaldo and vacation premium.

### The Solution: Employer of Record (EOR)

An EOR becomes your legal employer in Mexico on KE's behalf. KE pays the EOR a monthly fee, and the EOR handles all Mexican payroll, tax withholding, benefits, and compliance.

| EOR Provider | Mexico Coverage | Approximate Monthly Cost | Notes |
|-------------|----------------|--------------------------|-------|
| **Deel** | Yes | $499-599/month + statutory benefits | Large platform, strong Mexico presence |
| **Remote** | Yes | $599/month + statutory benefits | Good compliance reputation |
| **Oyster** | Yes | $499-699/month + statutory benefits | Designed for small orgs |
| **Papaya Global** | Yes | $650+/month | More enterprise-focused |

**What EOR costs look like in total:** Your salary + 25-35% for Mexican statutory benefits (IMSS, Infonavit, aguinaldo, prima vacacional) + $500-700/month EOR service fee. For example, on a $60K/year salary, the total cost to KE would be roughly $80-85K/year including all Mexican obligations and EOR fees.

### Without an EOR

KE would need to either:

1. **Register a Mexican branch or subsidiary** -- expensive ($5-15K setup, ongoing compliance costs) and complex for a small nonprofit
2. **Reclassify you as an independent contractor** -- risky if the working relationship looks like employment (full-time, exclusive, KE controls your schedule/tools). The IRS and SAT both scrutinize misclassification. Penalties fall on the organization.
3. **Do nothing and accept the risk** -- many small US orgs do this. The risk is low day-to-day but real if Mexican authorities ever look into it. As ED of a foundation with an active bioregion in Mexico (Veracruz), you have more visibility than a typical remote worker.

## 2. US Taxes

### What Doesn't Change

- You're a US citizen (or permanent resident), so you file US federal taxes on worldwide income regardless of where you live
- Social Security and Medicare taxes are still owed -- living abroad doesn't exempt you
- If KE pays you as a W-2 employee through a US payroll, federal withholding continues as normal

### What Changes

#### Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)

- You can exclude up to ~$126,500 (2025 figure, adjusted annually) of earned income from US federal income tax
- **To qualify**, you must meet either:
  - **Bona fide residence test:** You're a genuine resident of Mexico (not just visiting). Having a lease, local bank account, Mexican tax registration, and temporary/permanent residency all support this.
  - **Physical presence test:** You're outside the US for 330 full days in any 12-month period. "Full days" means midnight to midnight -- partial days in the US count against you.
- The FEIE reduces your income tax, but does NOT reduce Social Security or Medicare tax
- If KE has already withheld federal income tax, you claim the FEIE on your personal return and get a refund of the excess withheld
- **You must file Form 2555** with your tax return to claim the FEIE

#### Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)

- If you pay income tax to Mexico, you can credit that against your US tax liability to avoid being taxed twice on the same income
- **You cannot use both the FEIE and FTC on the same income.** Your CPA will determine which saves you more money based on your specific income level and Mexico tax rate.
- Generally, if your income is below the FEIE threshold, the FEIE is better. If above, or if Mexican taxes are high enough, the FTC may be better.

#### Foreign Housing Exclusion

- If you qualify for the FEIE, you may also exclude or deduct certain foreign housing costs (rent, utilities -- not food or clothing) above a base amount
- The base amount is roughly $18,000/year; you can exclude qualified housing costs above that, up to a cap that varies by location
- **Form 2555** covers this as well

#### State Taxes

- If you were a resident of a US state before moving, check that state's rules for ceasing residency. Most states stop taxing you once you've established domicile elsewhere, but some (California, New York, New Mexico, Virginia) are aggressive about continuing to claim former residents.
- **Vermont:** Generally reasonable. If you've genuinely left (no home, no driver's license, no voter registration, not returning regularly), Vermont should not continue to tax you. File a final part-year return for the year you left.
- **Cut ties cleanly:** Surrender your state driver's license, update your voter registration, close state bank accounts, change your mailing address. Every tie you maintain is an argument the state uses to keep taxing you.

#### FBAR (Foreign Bank Account Report)

- If the **aggregate balance** across ALL your foreign financial accounts (Mexican bank accounts, investment accounts, etc.) exceeds **$10,000 at any point during the year**, you must file FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) by April 15
- This is a reporting requirement, not a tax -- but penalties for non-filing are severe ($10,000+ per account per year for non-willful violations, up to $100,000 or 50% of account balance for willful violations)
- Filed electronically through the BSA E-Filing System, separate from your tax return

#### FATCA (Form 8938)

- If your foreign financial assets exceed **$200,000 at end of year** or **$300,000 at any point during the year** (thresholds for taxpayers living abroad, filing single), you must report them on **Form 8938** filed with your tax return
- This overlaps with FBAR but is a separate requirement with different thresholds

## 3. Mexico Taxes

### Tax Residency

Mexico considers you a tax resident if any of the following apply:

- You have established your **casa habitacion** (primary home) in Mexico
- Your **center of vital interests** is in Mexico (more than 50% of your income is sourced in Mexico, OR your primary professional activities are performed in Mexico)
- You are physically present in Mexico for **183 or more days** in a calendar year

As a tax resident, Mexico taxes your **worldwide income**.

### What You Owe

- **Income tax (ISR):** Progressive rates from 1.92% to 35%. On a $60K/year salary (~1.1M MXN at current rates), your effective Mexican rate would be roughly 20-25%.
- **If KE pays you through a US payroll** (no EOR), Mexico won't be withholding taxes automatically. You'd need to make **provisional monthly payments** (pagos provisionales) to SAT and file an annual return (Declaracion Anual) by April 30.
- **If KE uses an EOR**, the EOR withholds Mexican ISR from your pay and remits it to SAT. Much simpler.

### Registro Federal de Contribuyentes (RFC)

- You need an RFC to be a taxpayer in Mexico. This is Mexico's equivalent of a Social Security number / tax ID.
- If you're going through an EOR, they handle your registration. If not, you'll need to register at a SAT office with your passport, CURP (if you have one), proof of address, and residency card.

### Tax Treaty

The US-Mexico tax treaty prevents double taxation. In practice:

- You'll pay tax to both countries but get credit on one side for taxes paid to the other
- Your total tax burden will be approximately equal to whichever country's rate is higher -- not both added together
- A CPA experienced in both jurisdictions will optimize whether to use the FEIE or FTC on the US side

## 4. Visa and Immigration

### Visa Options

| Visa Type | Duration | Work Permitted? | How to Get It | Cost |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|---------------|------|
| **Tourist (FMM)** | Up to 180 days | No -- technically cannot work, even remotely for a foreign employer | Issued at port of entry | ~$30 USD (often included in airfare) |
| **Temporary Residency** | 1 year initially, renewable up to 4 years | Living in Mexico yes; working for a Mexican employer requires a separate work permit. Remote work for a foreign employer is a gray area. | Apply at Mexican consulate in the US, then complete at INM office in Mexico | ~$250-400 USD |
| **Temporary Residency with Work Permit** | Same as above | Yes, for a specific Mexican employer | Employer (or EOR) sponsors you through INM | ~$300-500 USD |
| **Permanent Residency** | Indefinite | Yes | After 4 years of temporary residency, or through family ties, or sufficient points (age, income, experience) | ~$400 USD |

### Which One You Need

- **If using an EOR:** The EOR is your legal employer in Mexico. They can sponsor your temporary residency with a work permit. This is the cleanest path.
- **If not using an EOR:** Temporary residency (without work permit) is the most common path for remote workers employed by foreign companies. Mexico has not aggressively enforced the "no work" provision against remote workers for foreign employers, but this is a known gray area and policies may tighten.
- **Do not stay on a tourist visa long-term.** Beyond the legal issues, a tourist visa prevents you from opening bank accounts, signing long-term leases, buying property, and accessing many services. Temporary residency is relatively straightforward to obtain.

### Obtaining Temporary Residency

1. **Apply at a Mexican consulate in the US** (or your country of citizenship). You'll need:
   - Valid passport (6+ months validity)
   - Proof of economic solvency: bank statements showing ~$2,500-5,000 USD/month income for the last 6 months, OR ~$40,000-75,000 USD in savings/investments (thresholds vary by consulate)
   - Completed application form
   - Passport photos
   - Application fee

2. **Once approved**, you receive a visa sticker in your passport. You then have 180 days to enter Mexico and complete the process at your local INM (Instituto Nacional de Migracion) office, where you'll receive your residency card.

3. **Renewal:** Your first temporary residency is for 1 year. Renewals are for 1-3 years. After 4 years of temporary residency, you can apply for permanent residency.

### Important Notes

- **Notify INM of address changes.** You're required to report your address and any changes within 30 days.
- **Don't overstay or violate visa terms.** Mexico's immigration enforcement is generally relaxed, but violations can result in fines, detention, or deportation orders that make re-entry difficult.
- **Carry your residency card.** Mexican law requires foreign residents to carry their immigration document at all times.

## 5. Land Ownership in Mexico

If you're considering buying property in Mexico -- either personally or for a future project:

### The Restricted Zone

- **Within 50 km of the coast or 100 km of an international border**, foreigners cannot directly own land. This is Mexico's "restricted zone" under Article 27 of the Constitution.
- **Workaround: Fideicomiso (bank trust).** A Mexican bank holds title to the property in trust for your benefit. You have full use, rental, and sale rights. The trust is renewable every 50 years. Setup cost is $1,500-3,000 USD, annual fees $500-1,000 USD.
- **Alternative: Mexican corporation.** If the property is for commercial or non-residential purposes, you can own it through a Mexican company (Sociedad Anonima or similar). The company can be 100% foreign-owned for non-residential land.

### Outside the Restricted Zone

- Foreigners can own land directly in fee simple, same as a Mexican citizen. No trust required.
- You must obtain a permit from the Secretaria de Relaciones Exteriores (SRE) and agree to the "Calvo Clause" -- you agree to be treated as a Mexican national regarding the property and not invoke foreign diplomatic protection.

### Ejido Land

- **Ejidos** are communal land holdings that cover a significant portion of Mexico's rural land. Ejido land **cannot be sold to foreigners** and has restrictions even on sales to Mexican citizens.
- Some ejido land has been "regularized" (converted to private property through the PROCEDE/FANAR programs), in which case normal ownership rules apply.
- **Be extremely careful** when buying rural land in Mexico. Verify the land title, confirm it is not ejido land, and use a notario publico (Mexican notary, who is a licensed attorney in Mexico) for all transactions.

### Due Diligence for Any Property Purchase

- **Use a notario publico.** In Mexico, notarios are government-appointed attorneys who authenticate real estate transactions. Their involvement is legally required for property transfers. They verify titles, liens, and legal status.
- **Get a title search** (certificado de libertad de gravamen) from the local Public Registry of Property
- **Verify the property's legal status** -- is it private property, ejido, federal land, or part of a natural protected area?
- **Confirm tax status** -- check for outstanding property taxes (predial) with the local municipality
- **Water rights:** In many parts of Mexico, water rights are separate from land ownership and must be obtained from CONAGUA (the national water authority)

### Property Taxes and Ongoing Costs

- **Predial (property tax):** Significantly lower than US property taxes -- often 0.1-0.3% of assessed value annually
- **Acquisition tax (ISAI):** One-time transfer tax of 2-5% of the transaction value (varies by state)
- **Notario fees:** 3-6% of the transaction value (covers their legal services, tax filings, and registration)
- **Capital gains tax on sale:** Mexico taxes capital gains on real estate at up to 35%. There are deductions and exemptions available, especially for primary residences.

## 6. Private Foundation Compliance (KE-Specific)

### Self-Dealing Rules (IRC 4941)

As Executive Director of a private foundation, you are a "disqualified person." This triggers strict rules about any transaction between you and KE:

- **Compensation is allowed** if it is "reasonable and necessary" for carrying out KE's exempt purposes
- **The board must formally approve your compensation** in a documented resolution. You should recuse yourself from the vote.
- **Reasonableness:** Your total compensation (salary + benefits + any housing/travel allowances + the cost of EOR services) should be comparable to what similar-sized foundations pay their executive directors. Document this with a comparability analysis -- even an informal one showing 3-5 comparable positions at similar organizations.
- **Travel reimbursement:** Expenses for KE business (bioregional activations, funder meetings, etc.) are fine. But if KE reimburses travel that is partly personal, the personal portion must be separated and not reimbursed.
- **If KE provides any housing benefit** (stipend, paid housing, etc.), it must be documented as part of your compensation package and included in the reasonableness analysis.

### Penalties for Self-Dealing

- **First-tier tax:** 10% of the amount involved, imposed on the disqualified person (you). 5% on any foundation manager who knowingly approved it.
- **Second-tier tax:** If not corrected within the taxable period, the tax increases to 200% on the disqualified person and 50% on the foundation manager.
- These taxes are **automatic** -- there is no intent requirement for the first-tier tax. If a self-dealing transaction occurred, the tax applies.

### Annual Compliance

- **Form 990-PF:** KE must file annually. Your compensation must be reported on Part VIII. This is a public document.
- **Schedule B:** Reports contributors of $5,000+. This is not public for private foundations.
- **Excise tax on net investment income:** Private foundations pay a 1.39% excise tax on net investment income annually.

### Board Governance While You're Abroad

- Ensure the board can meet (virtually is fine) and that your absence from the US doesn't create governance gaps
- Maintain regular board meeting minutes documenting oversight of the foundation's activities, including your compensation
- If you're the sole employee and primary decision-maker, the board's independent oversight role becomes even more important to demonstrate to the IRS

## 7. Banking and Money

### Mexican Bank Accounts

- **You need temporary or permanent residency to open a Mexican bank account.** Tourist visa holders generally cannot open accounts (some banks make exceptions for basic accounts).
- **Major banks:** BBVA Mexico, Banorte, Santander Mexico, Citibanamex (now owned by Grupo Mexico), HSBC Mexico
- **What you'll need to open an account:** Residency card, passport, RFC (tax ID), proof of address (utility bill or lease), and sometimes proof of income
- **Keep in mind:** Mexican banks have lower deposit insurance limits (IPAB covers ~$130,000 USD equivalent per institution). Interest on Mexican savings accounts is taxed.

### Getting Paid

- **If through EOR:** Paid in MXN to your Mexican bank account. Clean and simple.
- **If through US payroll:** Paid in USD to your US bank account. You'll transfer to Mexico as needed using:
  - **Wise (formerly TransferWise):** Best exchange rates, low fees, fast transfers
  - **Mercury / Relay:** US business banking that works well internationally
  - **Direct bank wire:** Works but expensive ($25-45 per transfer, plus unfavorable exchange rates)
  - **Avoid:** PayPal and Western Union for regular salary transfers (high fees, poor rates)

### FBAR Reminder

If your Mexican bank account(s) plus any other foreign accounts exceed $10,000 in aggregate at any point during the year, you must file the FBAR. This catches most people who open even a basic Mexican checking account.

### Practical Notes

- Private healthcare in Mexico is significantly cheaper than the US, even without insurance. A specialist visit is typically $30-80 USD, and private hospital stays are a fraction of US costs.
- Major cities (Mexico City, Guadalajara, Monterrey, Merida, Queretaro) have excellent private hospitals. Rural areas have limited options.
- Dental and vision care are dramatically cheaper in Mexico. Many expats pay out of pocket rather than insuring.

## 9. Impact on Kinship Earth Operations

### Foundation Registration

- If KE conducts activities in Mexico (bioregional activations, grantmaking to Mexican organizations, employing you in Mexico), Mexico may consider KE to be operating on Mexican soil.
- **Registro Federal de las Organizaciones de la Sociedad Civil:** Foreign nonprofits operating in Mexico should register with CLUNI (Clave Unica de Inscripcion) through the Registro Federal. This is not always enforced for light-touch activities but is technically required for sustained operations.
- **If KE makes grants to Mexican organizations or individuals,** be aware of both US (expenditure responsibility rules for private foundations making foreign grants) and Mexican tax implications.

### Foreign Grant Compliance (Expenditure Responsibility)

- As a private foundation, KE cannot make grants to non-US organizations as freely as a public charity can.
- **Expenditure responsibility (IRC 4945(h)):** For any grant to a non-US organization that is not equivalent to a US public charity, KE must:
  - Conduct a pre-grant inquiry into the grantee
  - Execute a written grant agreement with specific terms
  - Require regular progress reports
  - Report the grant on Form 990-PF
  - Ensure the funds are used for charitable purposes
- **Equivalency determination:** Alternatively, KE can obtain an equivalency determination (opinion of US counsel that the foreign grantee is equivalent to a US public charity). This is less burdensome than full expenditure responsibility.
- If Flow Funders are operating in Mexico, the grants to them should already comply with these rules.

### Perception and Funder Relations

- Some US funders may ask questions about why the ED of a US foundation lives abroad. This isn't a dealbreaker but should be addressed proactively.
- **Frame it positively:** You're embedded in one of KE's active bioregions. You're doing the work on the ground, not managing from a distance.
- **The Planetary Party Protocol has an active Mexican bioregion (Veracruz).** Your presence in Mexico directly serves the mission.

## 10. Practical Quality of Life

### Cost of Living

Mexico's cost of living is significantly lower than most of the US, which means your salary stretches further:

- **Rent:** $300-800/month for a comfortable 1-2 bedroom in most cities outside CDMX. $600-1,500 in Mexico City or tourist areas.
- **Food:** $200-400/month eating a mix of home-cooked and restaurants
- **Transportation:** $50-150/month (public transit, occasional taxi/Uber)
- **Utilities:** $50-100/month (electricity, water, gas, internet)
- **Total:** A comfortable life in Mexico can cost $800-2,000/month depending on location and lifestyle

### Safety

- Safety varies enormously by region. Research specific cities and neighborhoods.
- Broadly: major cities and tourist areas are comparable in safety to large US cities. Some rural and border areas have significant security concerns.
- The US State Department maintains travel advisories by Mexican state.

### Internet and Remote Work Infrastructure

- Major cities have reliable high-speed internet (50-200+ Mbps fiber is common)
- Coworking spaces are abundant in Mexico City, Guadalajara, Merida, Playa del Carmen, Oaxaca, San Miguel de Allende, and other expat-popular cities
- Smaller towns and rural areas can have unreliable connectivity -- important if your work requires constant video calls
