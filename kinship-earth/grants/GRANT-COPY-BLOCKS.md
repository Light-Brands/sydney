## 1. Organizational Overview

Kinship Earth is a 501(c)(3) private foundation that practices trust-based participatory grantmaking -- deploying unrestricted grants directly to vetted community leaders and place-based organizations who determine how resources are used. Founded in 2021 and led by Executive Director Sydney Griffith, Kinship Earth builds on a grantmaking lineage pioneered by Marion Rockefeller Weber in the early 1990s, when she entrusted unrestricted funds to Susan Davis Moora -- now a Kinship Earth co-founder -- to flow resources to the grassroots leaders she trusted most. Today, Kinship Earth serves as both financial infrastructure and education platform, working to transform how philanthropic capital reaches the communities and ecosystems that need it most.

## 2. Our Approach to Giving

Kinship Earth's grantmaking model -- which we call Flow Funding -- operates through a four-step cycle designed for trust, agility, and community self-determination. First, we identify trusted community leaders through a rigorous, invitation-based selection process led by our Flow Fund Advisors and leadership team, drawing on years of on-the-ground relationships across bioregions. Second, selected leaders receive unrestricted grants and exercise locally-determined resource allocation -- directing funds to real-time community priorities without submitting applications or requesting permission. Third, grantees share lightweight reports and narrative-based impact documentation, capturing what surprised, moved, challenged, and inspired them -- an accountability model that respects their time while giving donors a richer, more honest window into outcomes than traditional compliance reporting. Fourth, each cohort of grantees recommends the next round of trusted leaders, creating a self-perpetuating, peer-nominated network that grows through trust rather than top-down selection.

## 3. Impact to Date

Since launching its Flow Fund in mid-2024, Kinship Earth has raised over $800,000 and deployed more than $370,000 in unrestricted grants to 23 community leaders and organizations across 12+ bioregions globally -- spanning Cascadia, the Greater Tkaronto Bioregion, the Northeast of Turtle Island, Colombia, Jamaica, Mexico, and East Africa. Across three cohorts, these grants have catalyzed outcomes including the protection of sacred waterways from toxic mining in Minnesota, the purchase of food preservation infrastructure strengthening food sovereignty on reservations, the launch of community gardens that turned $1,000 in seeds into over $40,000 worth of food for low-income families, and the seeding of bioregional organizing networks coordinating regenerative action across entire watersheds. Kinship Earth has also received $300,000 in grants from the Novo Foundation, affirming the credibility and effectiveness of its model at a time when the broader philanthropic sector is increasingly embracing trust-based, participatory approaches to giving.

## 4. Our Role as Intermediary

Kinship Earth addresses a fundamental challenge in trust-based philanthropy: most donors -- even well-intentioned ones -- do not know who to trust, how to find the right people, or how to verify that resources are reaching the communities that need them most. We bridge that gap. Our leadership team has spent years cultivating trust-based relationships with grassroots leaders across 12+ bioregions -- a relational infrastructure that cannot be assembled overnight. When donors give through Kinship Earth, they access a rigorous, nomination-based vetting process, ongoing relational accountability through our Communities of Practice, and rich narrative documentation of outcomes -- all while ensuring that grantees spend their time doing the work, not writing grant reports. We steward both donors and grantees: donors are invited into shared learning spaces alongside the leaders their resources support, and grantees receive structured onboarding, peer connection, and ongoing support without the compliance burdens that drain grassroots capacity.

## 5. Bioregional Focus

Kinship Earth organizes its grantmaking by bioregion -- geographic areas defined by watersheds, ecosystems, and cultural communities rather than political boundaries. This place-based approach reflects a growing body of evidence that the most effective responses to interconnected environmental, social, and economic challenges are coordinated at the bioregional scale, where ecological systems, community relationships, and economic activity naturally intersect. By resourcing leaders who are deeply embedded in their bioregions, we fund people who naturally work across issue silos -- because a leader responding to food insecurity on a reservation is simultaneously advancing food sovereignty, cultural preservation, ecosystem health, and economic resilience. Each bioregional Flow Fund becomes a node in a larger decentralized infrastructure for systemic regeneration, and our five-year strategy aims to support the launch of 20+ autonomous, community-governed bioregional Flow Funds worldwide.

## 6. Vision and Field Building

Kinship Earth is not only a grantmaker -- it is a field-builder working to shift how philanthropy itself operates. Alongside deploying capital, we are developing a free, modular Bioregional Flow Funding Playbook to help communities anywhere in the world launch their own trust-based grantmaking vehicles. We educate family offices, donor-advised funds, and foundations on participatory approaches to giving, and we convene Communities of Practice where grantees, donors, and leadership learn together across bioregions and cultures. Our ten-year vision is to raise and deploy $200 million through this model -- not by centralizing control, but by serving as backbone infrastructure that provides legal, educational, and relational support to a growing network of place-based Flow Funds. We believe that when capital moves like water -- freely, wisely, and in relationship with the land and people it touches -- systemic healing becomes possible at a planetary scale.
