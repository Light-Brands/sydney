# The Case for Kinship Earth as Intermediary

**Why Give Through Kinship Earth Rather Than Directly to Grassroots Leaders?**

---

## The Short Answer

You could give directly to a grassroots leader. Some donors do, and we encourage it when the relationship and trust are already there. But most donors -- even well-intentioned ones -- face a fundamental challenge: **they don't know who to trust, how to find the right people, or how to verify that resources are reaching the communities and ecosystems that need them most.**

Kinship Earth exists to solve that problem. We are a trust-based intermediary that bridges the gap between philanthropic capital and the grassroots leaders, nonprofits, and bioregional organizing groups doing the most effective, most responsive, and most underfunded work on the planet.

What follows is the case for why that intermediary role matters -- and what Kinship Earth brings that most donors cannot easily replicate on their own.

---

## 1. We Solve the Discovery Problem: Our Flow Funder Selection Process

The single biggest challenge in trust-based philanthropy is identifying the right people. Kinship Earth has built a rigorous, relationship-driven vetting process that no individual donor can easily replicate.

### How We Find and Select Flow Funders

Our selection process is **invitation-driven, not application-driven.** We do not accept unsolicited funding requests. Instead, we rely on a layered process of nomination, evaluation, and onboarding:

- **Nominations come from trusted sources** -- our Flow Fund Advisors (grassroots changemakers actively working in the field), our Board of Advisors, our existing Flow Funders, and our leadership team. Each nominator brings years of on-the-ground relationships in their bioregion.

- **Candidates are evaluated against core criteria** -- including being deeply community-rooted, having a demonstrated track record of stewardship, relational orientation, strategic awareness, clarity of purpose, and ethical accountability. We also prioritize sectoral and demographic diversity, alignment with our impact areas, and cultural and linguistic fluency.

- **Anonymous scoring and group discussion** reduce bias. Final decisions are made by our Flow Fund Allocation Committee, which includes Kinship Earth's leadership and Flow Fund Advisors.

- **Conflict of interest safeguards** are built in. We ask nominators and participants in the vetting process to disclose potential conflicts -- for example, recommending individuals with whom they have direct financial relationships. Diverse voices and group discussion help reduce bias and foster shared accountability.

- **Onboarding is personal and thorough.** Every Flow Funder has a 1-on-1 onboarding call with our Executive Director, receives a detailed Role & Responsibilities document, and is invited to a group introductory call before receiving any funds. They sign a written agreement outlining expectations and are welcomed into our Community of Practice.

### Why This Matters for Donors

When you give to Kinship Earth, you are leveraging a vetting infrastructure that took years of relationship-building to construct. Our leadership and advisors have spent decades cultivating trust across bioregions -- a "deep Rolodex" built through boots-on-the-ground presence, not desk research. This network cannot be assembled overnight. It is the product of thousands of conversations, site visits, convenings, and shared work.

A donor writing a check directly to a grassroots leader they found online is making a bet. A donor giving through Kinship Earth is investing in a proven system of relational vetting backed by people who have earned the trust of communities across 12+ bioregions globally.

## 2. We Verify Impact Without Burdening Changemakers: Our "Trust But Verify" Approach

Traditional philanthropy imposes heavy compliance and reporting requirements that drain the time and energy of the very people doing the most important work. Kinship Earth takes a fundamentally different approach.

### Light-Touch Reporting

We ask Flow Funders for basic reports: who they gave funds to, why, and what happened. We do not require extensive grant narratives, itemized budgets, or quarterly compliance filings. Our Flow Funders are trusted leaders -- we treat them like it.

### Stories-Based Accountability

Inspired by Flow Funding pioneer Marion Rockefeller Weber, we invite Flow Funders to reflect on four simple but profound questions:

1. **What inspired you?**
2. **What moved you?**
3. **What surprised you?**
4. **What challenged you?**

These reflections -- shared through video, writing, voice notes, poetry, or any medium that feels natural -- form the backbone of our accountability model. They capture the depth and nuance of impact in ways that spreadsheets and compliance reports never could. They reveal not just *what* happened, but *why it mattered* -- both to the communities resourced and to the Flow Funders themselves.

### Verification Through Relationship

Light-touch reporting does not mean no verification. Our leadership maintains ongoing, direct relationships with every Flow Funder. Through 1-on-1 check-ins, Community of Practice gatherings, and ongoing dialogue, we have real-time visibility into how funds are moving and what impact is unfolding. This is relational accountability -- it is often more rigorous than paperwork because it is continuous and grounded in genuine relationship, not performative compliance.

### Why This Matters for Donors

As a donor, you receive rich, human stories of impact -- not sanitized reports written to satisfy a funder. You see the real texture of what your resources made possible. And you know that the people you're resourcing are spending their time doing the work, not writing grant reports. This approach respects grassroots leaders' time while giving donors a deeper, more honest window into impact than traditional reporting ever provides.

## 3. We Build Community, Not Just Portfolios: Stewardship and Communities of Practice

Kinship Earth does not simply write checks and wait for reports. We actively steward both our Flow Funders and our donors through ongoing community-building, shared learning, and mutual support.

### Communities of Practice

Every Flow Funder is welcomed into Kinship Earth's Community of Practice (CoP) -- a shared learning environment that brings together Flow Funders, our leadership team, and donors. Our CoP is built on co-created agreements (crafted together by the community), and includes:

- **Seasonal gatherings** (quarterly) for check-ins, story-sharing, and peer reflection
- **Deep-dive sessions** focused on themes like cross-cultural giving, bioregional funding design, or storytelling for impact
- **Peer coaching** where Flow Funders support one another through shared challenges
- **Collective reflection** at the close of each cycle, harvesting learnings across the community

The CoP ensures that learning flows in all directions -- from Flow Funders to leadership, from leadership to donors, and from Flow Funders to one another. It creates a living feedback loop that strengthens the model with each cycle.

### Stewardship of Flow Funders

We offer modest honorarium payments to Flow Funders who need them, recognizing that this work requires time, care, and effort. We maintain ongoing 1-on-1 relationships with each Flow Funder. If a Flow Funder cannot attend a Community of Practice gathering, we require a check-in via call or email -- because staying connected matters more than attendance.

### Stewardship of Donors

Donors are not treated as customers. They are co-stewards. We invite donors into our Community of Practice gatherings, share personal updates from the field, and offer visibility into where funds flow, why, and what outcomes emerge. Our quarterly online salons and annual in-person gatherings create space for dialogue and reflection -- not just fundraising asks.

### Why This Matters for Donors

When you give to Kinship Earth, you become part of a living network. You are connected to the Flow Funders your resources support, to other donors who share your values, and to a community of practice that is collectively learning how to practice philanthropy differently. This is not something you get by writing a check directly to a grassroots leader. This is the connective tissue that makes the whole system stronger.

## 5. Bioregionalism: Funding the Way Nature Organizes

Kinship Earth funds by bioregion, not by country, state, or issue area. This is a deliberate and strategic choice.

### What Bioregionalism Means

A bioregion is a geographic area defined by its natural features -- watersheds, soil types, plant communities, and cultural identities -- rather than political boundaries. Bioregional organizing brings together people who share a place and its ecological reality to collaborate on environmental, social, and economic challenges together.

### Why It Matters for Philanthropy

The crises we face -- ecological collapse, food insecurity, displacement, loss of biodiversity -- do not respect political borders. They follow ecological patterns. By organizing our funding along bioregional lines, we ensure that resources reach the natural systems and communities that constitute coherent, interconnected wholes.

Each Bioregional Flow Fund becomes a node in a larger decentralized infrastructure for planetary healing. Donors see impact in the form of stronger, healthier, more resilient bioregions -- not just isolated projects.

### Active Bioregions

Kinship Earth is currently active across:

- **Cascadia** (Pacific Northwest, USA & Canada)
- **Greater Tkaronto Bioregion** (in and around Toronto, Canada)
- **Northeast of Turtle Island** (New England & Mid-Atlantic, USA)
- **Cloud Forest Bioregion** (Mexico)
- **Montego Bay** (Jamaica)
- **Bioregions throughout Colombia**
- **East Africa**

### Why This Matters for Donors

By giving to Kinship Earth, your resources contribute to the health of whole bioregions -- not just individual projects. You fund the web of relationships and initiatives that, together, build bioregional resilience. This systems-level approach creates cascading impact that no single grant to a single organization can achieve.

## 6. Agility Over Compliance: Flow Funders Respond to What Communities Actually Need

This may be the most important point. In traditional philanthropy, a grassroots leader who receives a grant must use those funds for exactly what the grant says -- even if community needs have shifted since the application was written. The grant application itself takes months to write, pulling leaders away from the work. And the grant mandate forces conformity to what a foundation -- often thousands of miles away -- thinks should happen on the ground.

### How Kinship Earth Is Different

Our Flow Funders do not fill out grant applications. They do not submit proposals. They do not need to conform to what Kinship Earth's leadership thinks should happen on the ground. Instead:

- **Flow Funders receive unrestricted funds** and decide how, when, and where to move resources based on the emergent needs of their communities.
- **They can be agile.** If a crisis emerges -- a flood, a legal battle, a food shortage -- they can redirect resources immediately. They don't need to ask permission or submit a budget amendment.
- **Communities control decisions.** The people closest to the problem determine the solution. Not a program officer. Not a foundation board. The community.

### Real-World Examples

This agility has produced extraordinary outcomes:

- **WaterLegacy (Minnesota):** When a mining company threatened Lake Superior with toxic waste, Flow Funder Paula Maccabee used Kinship Earth funds to hire an expert who wrote a 36-page emergency opinion that helped stop construction. As she said: *"Kinship Earth gave us the courage to ask for what the community really needs in litigation -- that construction should stop until the courts make their final decision."* A traditional grant never could have responded this quickly or allowed this kind of pivot.

- **African Family Holistic Health Organization:** Flow Funder Je Amaechi's partners used funds to throw baby showers for immigrant and refugee mothers -- providing car seats, strollers, food, and community in a time of fear and scarcity. As Je reflected: *"I was surprised by how my own narrow focus on 'basic needs' had to expand. The community showed me a more complete picture."* No grant application would have anticipated or permitted this.

- **Farming Falmouth (Cape Cod):** $4,150 in flow funds were used to build soil with fresh compost for a service garden that grew over 5,000 lbs of food -- all donated to low-income families. The Flow Funder reflected: *"$1,000 of seeds will produce over $40,000 of food."*

- **Yusuf Offia Foundation (Nigeria):** $16,400 flowed to support food sovereignty for the internally displaced Ezza People across 12 villages, integrating agriculture with cultural preservation. The first harvest was so successful it catalyzed a second phase to replicate across 136 agrarian communities.

### Why This Matters for Donors

When you give to Kinship Earth, you are not locking resources into a predetermined plan that may no longer match reality by the time funds arrive. You are resourcing trusted leaders to respond in real time to what their communities actually need. This agility is one of the core reasons grassroots groups consistently outperform larger institutions -- and Kinship Earth's model is designed to protect and amplify it.

## A Different Kind of Intermediary

Kinship Earth is not a gatekeeper. We are a bridge.

We exist to connect philanthropic resources with the people who know best how to use them -- and to do so with the trust, accountability, and relational depth that this moment demands.

Traditional philanthropy locks up 93% of foundation assets in endowments. It forces grassroots leaders to spend their time writing applications instead of doing the work. It imposes mandates from thousands of miles away on communities that know exactly what they need.

Kinship Earth offers a different path: one where money flows like water -- freely, wisely, and regeneratively -- from donors who trust the model, through an intermediary that has earned the trust of communities, to the grassroots leaders who are closest to the solutions our planet needs.
