## **Slide 2 – Ecological Collapse and Deepening Inequality**

All around us, the signs are clear — our current systems are breaking down faster than they're being repaired.

This year alone, catastrophic floods in Brazil and the U.S., record-breaking wildfires across Canada and California, and wars in Gaza, Ukraine, and Sudan are displacing millions and devastating ecosystems.

These cascading crises signal that the status quo is not viable. What’s needed now is not incremental change — it’s planetary regeneration and systemic healing — transforming the systems that have harmed people and planet alike.

## **Slide 3 – Grassroots vs. Top-Down**

Here’s data that reflects the urgent global crises we’re facing today.

On the left, you can see that millions and billions of people are being affected by issues ranging from hunger and inadequate shelter to pollution and living in conflict zones.

On the right, when we compare top-down interventions to grassroots responses, grassroots groups consistently deliver more effective, more cost-efficient, and more responsive solutions.

Despite often operating on less than $50,000 a year, these groups outperform larger institutions in disaster response, conservation outcomes, and overall project success.

Funding grassroots action isn’t just ethical — it’s strategic.

Grassroots efforts originate from the bottom-up. They’re led by people directly impacted by an issue rather than centralized institutions. This work grows through trust, relationships, and collective action.

## **Slide 4 – Indigenous Biodiversity Gap**

Indigenous peoples protect 80% of the world’s biodiversity but receive less than 1% of climate finance.

This staggering imbalance underscores how institutional philanthropy often prioritizes centralized solutions that ignore or undermine local expertise.

To solve these issues, we must direct resources to those who have protected our planet for generations.

## **Slide 5 – Institutional Philanthropy Isn’t Meeting the Moment**

Institutional philanthropy often reinforces the inequities it aims to solve.

Funding distribution is often incongruent with what communities actually need. Bureaucracy and power imbalances perpetuate inequities and degradation.

Only about 7% of traditional foundation assets are distributed annually. That means 93% remains locked up while frontline communities wait.

The process is slow, compliance-heavy, and designed by people far removed from impacted communities.

Grassroots leaders are consistently underfunded. I’ve experienced this personally while co-founding Permatours. After four years of grant applications, we only received three grants.

Many promising regenerative efforts never receive the resources they need to grow.

## **Slide 6–7 – The Innovation of Flow Funding**

Flow Funding is the philanthropic approach Kinship Earth is taking.

### **Step 1:**

We identify trusted community leaders — individuals and grassroots organizations already making an impact. If they say yes to serving as a Flow Funder, we provide them unrestricted funds.

This first step is crucial because identifying trusted leaders is often the biggest challenge for donors.

### **Step 2:**

Flow Funders distribute funds locally without requiring applications. They determine the amount and timing based on emergent needs.

This reduces workload on change-makers and allows money to move when it’s actually needed.

### **Step 3:**

Flow Funders share basic reports and stories of impact — what surprised them, moved them, challenged them, inspired them.

This ensures accountability while reducing bureaucratic burden.

### **Step 4:**

Flow Funders recommend the next round of trusted leaders.

This creates a self-reinforcing ecosystem of trust.

We’re grateful to carry forward the legacy of Marion Rockefeller Weber, the pioneer of Flow Funding.

## **Slide 8 – Bioregional Flow Funding**

We fund both individual change-makers and bioregional organizing groups.

Bioregions are defined by ecosystems and cultural identities — not political boundaries.

Bioregional organizing involves collaboration at the intersection of environmental, social, and economic challenges.

Each Bioregional Flow Fund becomes a node in a larger decentralized infrastructure for planetary healing.

Donors eventually see ROI in the form of stronger, healthier, more resilient communities.

---

## **Slide 10–11 – Building Local Solutions Together**

Bioregional convenings and congresses support collective decision-making.

Participatory grant-making can be complex. Ego and fragmentation can emerge.

We mitigate risks by funding groups with trust-based structures.

Research shows groups often make better decisions than individuals. The “assembly bonus effect” demonstrates that collective intelligence can outperform even the most capable individual.

Example: The 2024 Ozarks Bioregional Congress strengthened networks, deepened ecological literacy, and advanced collaborative regeneration efforts.

## **Slides 14–15 – The Planetary Party & Flow Funding Playbook**

The Planetary Party is a global ecosystem activating bioregions through events and festivals.

Each gathering blends:

* Regenerative action  
* Cultural exchange  
* Systems mapping  
* Storytelling  
* Data collection  
* Flow Funding

Gatherings culminate in Bioregional Congresses where leaders allocate resources collectively.

We have created a modular Bioregional Flow Funding Playbook to help bioregional communities launch their own Flow Funds.

Together, the Planetary Party and Playbook form connective tissue for place-based action and global coordination.
