## Slide 2 — The Crisis Demands a New Approach

We are living through converging ecological, social, and economic crises that demand fundamentally different responses.

From devastating wildfires and floods to deepening inequality and mass displacement — the scale and speed of these challenges have outpaced our institutional capacity to respond.

What's needed is not incremental reform. It's a paradigm shift — in how we organize, how we resource communities, and how we allow capital to flow toward the people closest to these problems.

Kinship Earth exists because the people closest to the problem are closest to the solution. Our job is to resource them — with trust, speed, and accountability built into the relationship, not into bureaucracy.

---

## Slide 3 — Grassroots Leaders Outperform Top-Down Systems

The data is clear: grassroots organizations consistently deliver more effective, more cost-efficient, and more culturally responsive outcomes than top-down interventions.

Despite often operating on less than $50,000 a year, community-led groups outperform larger institutions in disaster response, conservation, and long-term project success.

Funding grassroots leadership is both a moral imperative and a strategic investment. These groups multiply the impact of every dollar because they eliminate overhead, bureaucracy, and misalignment between funders and communities.

This is why a growing body of evidence — and a growing number of leading foundations — are embracing trust-based, participatory approaches to giving.

## Slide 4 — The Indigenous Biodiversity Gap

Indigenous peoples steward 80% of the world's remaining biodiversity — yet receive less than 1% of climate finance.

This disparity represents one of the greatest missed opportunities in philanthropy. Indigenous and frontline communities hold centuries of ecological knowledge and proven stewardship practices.

Kinship Earth is working to close this gap by directing resources to Indigenous-led initiatives across bioregions — from land rematriation efforts to food sovereignty programs to cultural preservation and treaty rights advocacy.

---

## Slide 5 — Institutional Philanthropy Isn't Meeting the Moment

Traditional philanthropy — despite good intentions — often reinforces the inequities it aims to solve.

Only about 7% of foundation assets are distributed annually. That means 93% of philanthropic capital remains locked up in endowments while frontline communities wait for resources.

The process is slow, compliance-heavy, and designed by people far removed from impacted communities. Grassroots leaders spend more time writing grant applications than doing the work they're uniquely positioned to do. I've experienced this personally — after four years of grant applications while co-founding Permatours, we received only three grants.

Kinship Earth offers a proven alternative: trust-based participatory grantmaking that moves money when and where it's actually needed — with dignity, speed, and relational accountability.

## Slides 6–7 — How Flow Funding Works

Kinship Earth practices trust-based participatory grantmaking — what we call Flow Funding — building on a model pioneered by Marion Rockefeller Weber in the early 1990s. Here is how it works:

### Step 1: Identify Trusted Leaders

We identify trusted community leaders — individuals and grassroots organizations already making a measurable impact — through a rigorous, invitation-based selection process. Candidates are nominated by our Flow Fund Advisors, Board of Advisors, existing Flow Funders, and leadership team — drawing on years of on-the-ground relationships across bioregions. Candidates are evaluated against core criteria, scored by an independent allocation committee, and onboarded through a structured process before receiving any funds.

This first step addresses one of the biggest challenges for donors and DAFs: how to identify high-impact, trustworthy grassroots leaders in communities you may not have direct access to. When you give through Kinship Earth, you leverage a vetting infrastructure that took years of relationship-building to construct.

### Step 2: Community-Directed Resource Deployment

Selected leaders receive unrestricted grants and exercise locally-determined resource allocation — directing funds to real-time community priorities without submitting applications or requesting permission. They determine the amount, timing, and use based on what their communities actually need, when they need it.

This adaptive approach enables rapid response to shifting conditions — whether a sudden environmental threat, a food security gap, or an opportunity for land stewardship — without the delays inherent in traditional grant amendment processes.

### Step 3: Narrative-Based Impact Reporting

Flow Funders share reports and narrative-based impact documentation — capturing what surprised, moved, challenged, and inspired them. This accountability model replaces cumbersome compliance with relational accountability while giving donors a richer, more honest window into outcomes than traditional reporting.

Each grant cycle produces Community Impact Narratives alongside basic quantitative reporting: funds deployed, individuals and communities served, geographic reach, and impact area alignment. All documentation is available to donors and grantors upon request.

### Step 4: Trust Network Expansion

Flow Funders recommend the next round of trusted leaders — creating a self-perpetuating, peer-nominated network that grows through trust rather than top-down selection. This decentralized growth model means the people closest to the work identify who should receive resources next.

We carry forward the legacy of Marion Rockefeller Weber, the pioneer of Flow Funding, who envisioned philanthropy rooted in trust and relationships rather than excessive compliance.

## Slide 8 — Bioregional Flow Funding: Scaling Trust

Kinship Earth funds both individual change-makers and bioregional organizing groups.

Bioregions are defined by watersheds, ecosystems, and cultural communities — not political boundaries. This place-based approach reflects a growing body of evidence that the most effective responses to interconnected environmental, social, and economic challenges are coordinated at the bioregional scale — where ecological systems, community relationships, and economic activity naturally intersect.

Each Bioregional Flow Fund becomes a node in a larger decentralized infrastructure for community-led regeneration. This model is designed to scale: as each bioregion develops its own Flow Fund, Kinship Earth provides seed funding, mentorship, and our Flow Funding Playbook to ensure consistency, accountability, and impact.

For funders and DAFs, Bioregional Flow Funding offers a clear pathway to direct charitable resources toward specific geographies, communities, and impact areas — with built-in accountability through Kinship Earth's oversight and reporting.

## Slides 10–11 — Building Local Solutions Together

Bioregional convenings and congresses support participatory decision-making — bringing together community leaders, Indigenous wisdom keepers, and grassroots organizers to collectively allocate resources.

Research shows groups often make better decisions than individuals. The "assembly bonus effect" demonstrates that collective intelligence outperforms even the most capable individual decision-maker.

We mitigate the risks of participatory grantmaking by funding groups with established trust-based structures and demonstrated community accountability. Our selection process includes anonymous scoring, group discussion, and conflict of interest safeguards — reducing bias and fostering shared accountability.

## Slide 12 — Integrated Bioregional Impact Framework

Our Flow Funding supports charitable work across six interconnected impact areas. Environmental degradation, social inequity, and economic exclusion are not separate problems — they are interconnected symptoms of the same systemic failures. A community facing water contamination is simultaneously facing a health crisis, an economic crisis, and an environmental crisis.

Our six impact dimensions:

- **Ecological Restoration & Stewardship** — Regeneration of natural systems, watershed protection, biodiversity conservation, sustainable land management
- **Indigenous Rights & Land Stewardship** — Supporting sovereignty, land rematriation, treaty rights, and traditional ecological knowledge
- **Food Sovereignty & Basic Needs Security** — Community-led food systems, housing stability, and essential resource provision
- **Social & Economic Equity** — Justice, inclusion, mutual aid, community wealth-building, and equitable access to opportunity
- **Community Resilience & Disaster Response** — Preparedness, rapid response, and long-term recovery
- **Community & Ecosystem Health** — Holistic wellbeing, clean water, and the health of living systems

Rather than funding isolated interventions in one issue area, your investment supports leaders who work across these interconnected dimensions — producing compounding, systemic impact that siloed grantmaking cannot achieve.

## Slide 13 — Proven Impact: Stories from the Field

Here are examples of what trust-based, unrestricted grantmaking makes possible:

**WaterLegacy (Minnesota):** A $10,000 grant enabled WaterLegacy to hire a legal expert and file a 36-page emergency opinion to stop construction of a failing tailings dam threatening Lake Superior. The Minnesota Court of Appeals ruled in their favor, calling the state's original decision "arbitrary and capricious." As WaterLegacy's Paula Maccabee said: *"Kinship Earth gave us the courage to ask for what the community really needs in litigation — that construction should stop until the courts make their final decision."* A traditional grant never could have responded this quickly or allowed this kind of pivot.

**Farming Falmouth (Cape Cod):** A $4,150 grant built soil for a community service garden that grew over 5,000 lbs of food — all donated to low-income families. As the funder reflected: *"$1,000 of seeds will produce over $40,000 of food."*

**Yusuf Offia Foundation (Nigeria):** $16,400 across two phases is restoring food sovereignty for the internally displaced Ezza People across 12 villages, integrating agriculture with cultural preservation. The model is now being replicated across 136 agrarian communities.

**HoopDads (Tulsa):** A $5,000 grant expanded a fatherhood mentorship program that uses basketball to engage young dads — creating networks of accountability and strengthening entire families.

**African Family Holistic Health Organization:** Flow Funder Je Amaechi's partners used funds to throw baby showers for immigrant and refugee mothers — providing car seats, strollers, and community in a time of fear. As Je reflected: *"Flow Funding isn't just about mitigating harm; it's also about fueling joy, desire, and the audacity to create... even in hard times."*

Small, trust-based investments create extraordinary, lasting change because there is no red tape between the resources and the people who need them.

## Slides 14–15 — The Planetary Party & Flow Funding Playbook

Kinship Earth is the fiscal sponsor and founding partner of the Planetary Party — a global community engagement process designed to help bioregions self-organize, identify shared priorities, and launch their own Flow Funds.

Each Planetary Party gathering blends:
- Regenerative action and ecological literacy
- Cultural exchange and storytelling
- Systems mapping and community needs assessment
- Resource allocation and Flow Funding activation

Gatherings culminate in Bioregional Congresses where leaders collectively allocate resources to community-identified priorities.

We have also created a modular **Bioregional Flow Funding Playbook** — a free, replicable framework that enables any bioregional community to launch, manage, and sustain its own Flow Fund with accountability and transparency. We are also developing a companion playbook specifically for funders and DAFs interested in directing grants through the Flow Funding model.

Together, the Planetary Party and Playbook form the connective infrastructure for place-based charitable action and global coordination.
