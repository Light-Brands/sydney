# Bioregional Storytelling Deck Outline

**Kinship Earth -- Storytelling as Infrastructure for Bioregional Resourcing**

*How we amplify the work happening in bioregions to attract resources, deepen relationships, and build a movement.*

---

## Slide 1 -- Title

**Bioregional Storytelling: From Flow Funding to Film**

Kinship Earth's integrated approach to resourcing, activating, and amplifying bioregional work.

---

## Slide 2 -- The Opportunity

- Kinship Earth already flows unrestricted funds to trusted leaders across 12+ bioregions
- The work happening on the ground is extraordinary -- but most of it is invisible to the philanthropic world
- **The gap:** Bioregional organizers are doing world-class regenerative work but lack the storytelling capacity to attract the resources they deserve
- **The opportunity:** Kinship Earth is uniquely positioned to close this gap -- not by telling their stories for them, but by resourcing storytelling infrastructure that serves everyone

---

## Slide 3 -- The Three-Part Cadence

**Flow -- Activate -- Film**

Kinship Earth's integrated cycle for each bioregion:

1. **Flow Funds** -- Deploy unrestricted capital to trusted bioregional leaders and organizing groups
2. **Bioregional Activation Series** -- Convene, map, and weave the bioregion through online sessions, in-person gatherings, and distributed hub events
3. **Film & Story Capture** -- Document the activation and the ongoing bioregional work through professional and community-sourced content

Each phase feeds the next. Flowing funds seeds the work. Activations create gathering points for relationship and visibility. Film captures what emerges and attracts the next round of resources.

---

## Slide 4 -- Why Storytelling Matters for Bioregional Resourcing

- Donors give to what they can see and feel -- stories create that connection
- Bioregional work is inherently place-based, intersectional, and relational -- it resists reduction to metrics alone
- Kinship Earth's story-based accountability model (inherited from Marion Rockefeller Weber) already asks Flow Funders to reflect on what inspired, moved, surprised, and challenged them
- **Storytelling is not marketing -- it is the natural extension of our accountability practice, scaled up for broader impact**

---

## Slide 5 -- Kinship Earth's Role as Storytelling Intermediary

Just as Kinship Earth bridges donors and grassroots leaders financially, we bridge bioregional work and the wider world through storytelling:

| What We Already Do | What We're Building |
|---|---|
| Flow funds to bioregional leaders | Storytelling infrastructure alongside funding |
| Bioregional Activation Series (NE Turtle Island, Mexico) | Film capture integrated into every activation |
| Story-based reporting from Flow Funders | Aggregated content library across bioregions |
| Communities of Practice for shared learning | Shared storytelling assets that bioregions and Kinship Earth both use to attract support |

**Key principle:** Bioregional groups own their stories. Kinship Earth helps produce, aggregate, and distribute content -- but the voice and narrative belong to the communities.

## Slide 6 -- The Storytelling Model

### Two Content Streams

**Stream 1: Activation Series Film**
- Professional documentation of each Bioregional Activation Series
- Captures the gathering process: online prep sessions, in-person convenings at anchor sites, distributed hub gatherings
- Produces short-form content (2-5 min pieces for social/fundraising), mid-form documentary segments (10-20 min), and long-form documentary potential
- Shows the movement in motion -- people convening, mapping, governing, celebrating, building together

**Stream 2: Local Filmmaker Partnerships**
- Partner with filmmakers already embedded in each bioregion
- Source existing footage and content that tells the story of what's already happening on the ground
- Commission new content from local creators who know the land, the people, and the context
- Compensate local filmmakers fairly -- this is also a way to flow resources into creative economies in each bioregion

## Slide 8 -- What We Capture

Content themes aligned with the six pillars of the Bioregional Activation Series:

1. **Relationship-Building & Mapping** -- Who is already doing the work? How are they finding each other?
2. **Governance Education** -- Communities practicing shared decision-making and commons-based governance
3. **Tools, Tech & Frameworks** -- The platforms and coordination tools supporting bioregional organizing
4. **Healing & Conflict Transformation** -- How communities navigate friction and build trust across difference
5. **Honoring Place** -- Indigenous traditions, land stewardship, ecological knowledge rooted in specific bioregions
6. **Culture, Arts & Creative Expression** -- Music, ceremony, visual art, and storytelling as cultural infrastructure

Plus: **Flow Funder stories of impact** -- the four reflection questions (What inspired you? What moved you? What surprised you? What challenged you?) captured on film

## Slide 9 -- The Cadence in Practice

### Year 1 Bioregion Example

**Months 1-2: Flow Funds Deployed**
- Unrestricted grants reach bioregional leaders and organizing groups
- Initial relationship-building with local filmmakers
- Identify existing content and footage already collected in the bioregion

**Months 3-5: Bioregional Activation Series**
- Online preparation sessions (filmed/recorded)
- In-person gatherings at anchor sites (professional film crew + local filmmaker collaboration)
- Distributed hub gatherings (local filmmakers capture at partner sites across the bioregion)

**Months 5-8: Ongoing Story Capture**
- Local filmmakers continue documenting the work as it unfolds post-activation
- Flow Funders share video reflections using the four questions
- Community members contribute their own perspectives and footage

**Months 8-12: Content Production & Distribution**
- Kinship Earth post-produces and packages content for multiple uses
- Short-form pieces released for fundraising campaigns and social media
- Mid-form documentary segments shared with donors and at events
- All content shared back to bioregional groups for their own use
- Content informs the next funding cycle and attracts new resources

## Slide 11 -- Strengthening Our Intermediary Value

Storytelling directly strengthens Kinship Earth's role as intermediary:

**For Donors & Foundations:**
- See and feel the impact of trust-based giving through authentic, place-based stories
- Understand the bioregional lens -- why funding by ecosystem rather than issue area creates cascading impact
- Build emotional connection to the communities and leaders they're resourcing

**For Bioregional Groups:**
- Receive professional storytelling assets without diverting their own time and resources from the work
- Gain visibility with funders and supporters they would not otherwise reach
- Build their own fundraising and communications capacity over time

**For the Broader Movement:**
- Aggregate stories across bioregions to show the pattern -- flow funding works, bioregional organizing works, trust-based philanthropy works
- Create a living archive of the movement as it grows
- Inspire replication -- other bioregions see what's possible and want to join

## Slide 12 -- Working with Local Filmmakers

### Partnership Model

- **Identify filmmakers already embedded in each bioregion** -- people who know the land, the communities, and the cultural context
- **Source existing content** -- many filmmakers have already been documenting regenerative work, land stewardship, and community organizing in their bioregions. This footage is often sitting unused
- **Commission new work** -- structured shoots during activations and ongoing documentation between gatherings
- **Fair compensation** -- filmmakers are paid for their work. This is another way Kinship Earth flows resources into bioregional creative economies
- **Shared ownership** -- content agreements that give both the filmmaker and the bioregional community rights to use the material for their own purposes

### Why Local Filmmakers Matter
- They bring cultural fluency and trust that an outside crew cannot
- They can continue capturing stories long after a one-time activation event
- They build local storytelling capacity that outlasts any single project
- They are part of the community -- this is participatory storytelling, not extractive documentation

## Slide 16 -- The Ask

### What We Need to Launch Bioregional Storytelling

1. **Dedicated storytelling budget** integrated into bioregional activation funding
2. **Local filmmaker partnerships** in the Northeast and Mexico (identification and onboarding)
3. **Content infrastructure** -- simple systems for aggregating, organizing, and distributing content across Kinship Earth and bioregional partners
4. **A storytelling lead** or dedicated capacity within Kinship Earth to coordinate film production, manage filmmaker relationships, and oversee content strategy

### What This Unlocks

- A repeatable model for storytelling-integrated bioregional resourcing
- Professional content assets that serve Kinship Earth's fundraising and every bioregion's independent fundraising
- A growing library of movement stories that compound in value over time
- Stronger donor relationships built on authentic, place-based connection to impact

## Slide 17 -- Closing

**Kinship Earth flows funds. We activate bioregions. We tell the stories of what's happening on the ground.**

This is not storytelling as an add-on. It is storytelling as infrastructure -- as essential to bioregional resourcing as the funds themselves.

When donors can see the work, feel the relationships, and understand the place, they give more, give again, and bring others with them.

When bioregional groups have professional content they can use, they attract their own resources and reduce their dependency on any single intermediary.

When the movement has a living archive of its own growth, it becomes undeniable.

**We invite you to resource bioregional storytelling as a core part of the work.**
