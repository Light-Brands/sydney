# Kinship Earth: Donor & Grantor-Friendly Language Guide

**Purpose:** This document translates Kinship Earth's internal language into terminology that resonates with foundations, DAFs, institutional funders, and traditional grantors -- without losing the philosophical integrity of the work. It is designed as a reference for grant applications, LOIs, pitch decks, and donor communications.

---

## 1. Reframing "Flow Funding"

**The challenge:** "Flow Funding" is meaningful internally and carries the legacy of Marion Rockefeller Weber, but it can sound unfamiliar or abstract to program officers and foundation boards who are scanning for recognizable frameworks.

### Recommended Alternatives

| Context | Alternative Phrasing | When to Use |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| Grant applications & LOIs | **Trust-Based Participatory Grantmaking** | When applying to foundations that already use "trust-based philanthropy" language (e.g., Ford, Hewlett, MacArthur network) |
| DAF advisors & family offices | **Community-Directed Unrestricted Grantmaking** | When speaking to donor-advised fund holders who want to understand the mechanism |
| Impact-oriented funders | **Responsive Community Grantmaking** | When the funder cares about agility, speed, and real-time community responsiveness |
| Institutional / government | **Participatory Regrantmaking** | When the audience is accustomed to fiscal sponsorship and regrantmaking structures |
| Climate & conservation funders | **Place-Based Regenerative Grantmaking** | When applying to environmental or climate funders who value bioregional and place-based approaches |

### How to Introduce It

Instead of leading with "Flow Funding" as a branded term, lead with the recognized framework and then name it:

> **Before:** "Kinship Earth practices Flow Funding -- a model where unrestricted funds flow to trusted community leaders."
>
> **After:** "Kinship Earth practices trust-based participatory grantmaking -- deploying unrestricted grants directly to vetted community leaders and place-based organizations who determine how resources are used. We call this approach Flow Funding, building on a model pioneered by Marion Rockefeller Weber in the early 1990s."

This positions Flow Funding as Kinship Earth's specific implementation of a recognized philanthropic methodology, rather than an unfamiliar concept the reader must decode.

### Supporting Language

- "Our grantmaking model is **invitation-driven, not application-driven** -- reducing administrative burden on grassroots leaders while maintaining rigorous relational vetting."
- "We practice **unrestricted, community-directed grantmaking** organized by bioregion, ensuring resources reach the leaders closest to the challenges and solutions."
- "Our model draws on the growing body of evidence that **trust-based, participatory approaches** to philanthropy deliver more responsive, cost-effective, and equitable outcomes than traditional compliance-driven grantmaking."

## 2. Reframing "Emergent Needs"

**The challenge:** "Emergent needs" can sound vague or even risky to funders who want to know what their money will do. Foundations and DAFs are accustomed to proposals with defined outcomes. The concept that grantees can deploy funds "however they see fit" -- while core to the model -- needs to be framed as a *strategic advantage*, not an absence of strategy.

### Recommended Alternatives

| Internal Term | Donor-Friendly Alternative |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| Emergent needs | **Real-time community priorities** |
| Responding to emergent needs | **Adaptive, community-responsive deployment** |
| Deploy funds however they see fit | **Locally-determined resource allocation** |
| No grant applications | **Invitation-based selection with relational vetting** |
| No restrictions on use | **Unrestricted grants within defined impact areas** |

### How to Frame It

The key reframe: this isn't a lack of accountability -- it's a *more effective form of accountability* that trusts the expertise of people closest to the work.

> **Before:** "Our grantees respond to the emergent needs of their communities. We don't have grant applications or require them to do specific things. They deploy funds however they see fit."
>
> **After:** "Our grantees are embedded community leaders with deep knowledge of local conditions. Through unrestricted grants within our defined impact areas, they exercise **locally-determined resource allocation** -- directing funds to **real-time community priorities** as conditions evolve. This adaptive approach enables rapid response to shifting conditions -- whether a sudden environmental threat, a food security gap, or an opportunity for land stewardship -- without the delays inherent in traditional grant amendment processes."

### Supporting Evidence Language

When funders need reassurance, pair the concept with accountability:

- "While our grants are unrestricted, our **selection process is rigorous**: candidates are nominated by trusted advisors with deep bioregional ties, evaluated against defined criteria, scored by an independent allocation committee, and onboarded through a structured process before receiving any funds."
- "Grantees operate within Kinship Earth's **six defined impact areas** (detailed below), ensuring alignment with our charitable mission while preserving the agility that makes grassroots response so effective."
- "Research consistently demonstrates that **community-proximate decision-making** produces more cost-effective outcomes than centralized allocation. Our grantees' ability to respond to real-time conditions -- rather than adhering to a proposal written months earlier -- is a core strategic advantage of the model."

## 3. Reframing "Stories of Impact"

**The challenge:** "Stories of impact" can sound informal or anecdotal to funders who expect quantitative evidence. The term needs to convey that this is a deliberate, structured accountability practice -- not just storytelling.

### Recommended Alternatives

| Context | Alternative Phrasing |
|---------|---------------------|
| Grant reports & LOIs | **Community Impact Narratives** |
| Outcome-focused funders | **Qualitative Impact Documentation** |
| Evaluation-oriented audiences | **Narrative-Based Outcome Reporting** |
| Annual reports & donor updates | **Field Reports from Community Leaders** |
| Formal evaluation contexts | **Participatory Impact Assessment** |

### How to Frame It

Position the narrative approach as a *methodological choice* grounded in evidence, not a shortcut:

> **Before:** "We collect stories of impact from our Flow Funders."
>
> **After:** "We employ **narrative-based outcome reporting** -- a structured qualitative methodology in which grantees document community outcomes through guided reflections, field reports, and multimedia documentation. This approach captures the depth, context, and interconnected outcomes that quantitative metrics alone cannot convey, while respecting grantees' time and centering their expertise as the primary evaluators of community impact."

### The Four Guided Reflection Questions (Reframed)

Kinship Earth's four reflection questions -- inherited from Marion Rockefeller Weber -- can be presented as a structured evaluation framework:

| Original Framing | Donor-Friendly Framing |
|------------------|----------------------|
| What inspired you? | **Motivation & Alignment:** What community conditions or opportunities prompted your resource allocation decisions? |
| What moved you? | **Observed Outcomes:** What changes, results, or community responses did you witness as a direct result of the funded activities? |
| What surprised you? | **Emergent Learnings:** What unexpected outcomes, challenges, or insights arose during the grant period? |
| What challenged you? | **Adaptive Capacity:** What obstacles did you encounter, and how did you adapt your approach in response? |

### Combining Qualitative and Quantitative

For funders who require data, pair narrative reporting with lightweight metrics:

- "Each grant cycle produces **Community Impact Narratives** alongside basic quantitative reporting: funds deployed, number of individuals/communities served, geographic reach, and impact area alignment."
- "Our reporting model is informed by growing consensus in the philanthropic sector that **trust-based accountability** -- combining lightweight quantitative data with rich qualitative documentation -- reduces grantee burden while producing more honest, useful, and actionable impact data than traditional compliance-driven reporting."

## 4. Reframing Impact Categories Through a Bioregional & Intersectional Lens

**The challenge:** Kinship Earth's current impact areas (regeneration, social equity, economic equity, disaster preparedness, land rematriation, ecosystem health) are listed as separate categories, but the entire philosophy of the work is that these issues are deeply interconnected. Presenting them as silos contradicts the model. The reframe should emphasize bioregionalism as the organizing principle and intersectionality as the strategic rationale.

### The Core Argument for Funders

> Environmental degradation, social inequity, and economic exclusion are not separate problems -- they are interconnected symptoms of the same systemic failures, and they compound one another. A community facing water contamination is simultaneously facing a health crisis, an economic crisis, and an environmental crisis. Siloed funding that addresses only one dimension produces incomplete, often unsustainable results.
>
> Kinship Earth's bioregional approach recognizes this reality. By resourcing **place-based leaders who are embedded in their communities**, we fund people who naturally work across these boundaries -- because that is how community life actually operates. A leader responding to food insecurity on a reservation is simultaneously advancing food sovereignty, cultural preservation, ecosystem health, and economic resilience. Our model trusts that leader to allocate resources across these interconnected needs as conditions require.

### Reframed Impact Framework

Instead of listing six separate categories, present them as an integrated framework:

---

**Kinship Earth's Integrated Bioregional Impact Framework**

Kinship Earth resources place-based changemakers and bioregional organizations working at the intersection of environmental, social, and economic wellbeing. Our grantees address the following interconnected dimensions of community resilience:

| Impact Dimension | Description | How It Connects |
|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Ecological Restoration & Stewardship** | Regeneration of natural systems, watershed protection, biodiversity conservation, sustainable land management | Healthy ecosystems underpin food security, economic opportunity, cultural continuity, and community health |
| **Indigenous Rights & Land Stewardship** | Supporting Indigenous sovereignty, land rematriation, treaty rights, and traditional ecological knowledge | Indigenous-led stewardship protects biodiversity, sustains cultural identity, and models regenerative governance |
| **Food Sovereignty & Basic Needs Security** | Community-led food systems, housing stability, nutrition access, and essential resource provision | Food and housing security are prerequisites for community participation, health, and economic stability |
| **Social & Economic Equity** | Advancing justice, inclusion, mutual aid, community wealth-building, and equitable access to opportunity | Social cohesion and economic participation strengthen a bioregion's capacity to respond to environmental and humanitarian challenges |
| **Community Resilience & Disaster Response** | Preparedness, rapid response, and long-term recovery for climate events, displacement, and community crises | Resilience is built through the same networks, trust, and local knowledge that drive ecological and social outcomes |
| **Community & Ecosystem Health** | Human health, mental wellbeing, clean air and water, and the health of living systems | Health outcomes are inseparable from environmental quality, social equity, and economic access |

**Why this matters to funders:** Rather than funding isolated interventions in one issue area, your investment supports leaders who work across these interconnected dimensions -- producing compounding, systemic impact that siloed grantmaking cannot achieve.

---

### The Bioregional Rationale (For Funder Communications)

> "Kinship Earth organizes its grantmaking by **bioregion** -- geographic areas defined by watersheds, ecosystems, and cultural communities rather than political boundaries. This place-based approach reflects a growing body of evidence that **the most effective responses to interconnected environmental, social, and economic challenges are coordinated at the bioregional scale** -- the level at which ecological systems, community relationships, and economic activity naturally intersect.
>
> By investing in trusted leaders who are deeply embedded in their bioregions, Kinship Earth ensures that resources reach the people with the most comprehensive understanding of local conditions -- and the relationships necessary to coordinate effective, adaptive responses across issue areas."

### Positioning Intersectionality as Strategy

For funders who think in terms of "theory of change":

> **Theory of Change:** Environmental degradation, social inequity, and economic exclusion are interconnected and mutually reinforcing. Interventions that address only one dimension produce incomplete results. Kinship Earth resources community-embedded leaders who work across these dimensions simultaneously -- because that is how effective community change actually happens. By deploying unrestricted grants to trusted bioregional leaders, we enable adaptive, integrated responses that produce cascading benefits across environmental, social, and economic outcomes.

## 6. Framing for Specific Funder Types

### For Private Foundations

Lead with: **"Trust-based participatory grantmaking"** -- this is the language of the Trust-Based Philanthropy Project, which many foundations are already engaging with. Emphasize the rigorous vetting process, the structured accountability model, and the evidence base for unrestricted giving.

### For Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)

Lead with: **"Community-directed unrestricted grantmaking"** -- DAF advisors want to know their recommendation will produce real impact. Emphasize the bioregional model, the vetting infrastructure that took years to build, and the narrative impact reporting that gives donors a rich window into outcomes.

### For Corporate Foundations

Lead with: **"Place-based regenerative grantmaking with measurable community outcomes"** -- corporate funders often need metrics and geography. Emphasize the bioregional map, the quantitative data alongside qualitative narratives, and the specific communities being served.

### For Government Grants / Institutional Funders

Lead with: **"Participatory regrantmaking to community-based organizations"** -- use the language of fiscal sponsorship, regrantmaking, and community-based organizations. Emphasize the 501(c)(3) infrastructure, compliance processes, and structured reporting.

### For Climate & Environmental Funders

Lead with: **"Bioregional, place-based grantmaking at the intersection of ecological restoration and community resilience"** -- emphasize that environmental outcomes are inseparable from social and economic conditions, and that bioregional organizing is the most effective scale for integrated ecological stewardship.

### For Social Justice / Equity Funders

Lead with: **"Participatory, trust-based grantmaking centering community self-determination"** -- emphasize that communities control resource allocation, that the model decenters funder power, and that grantees include Indigenous leaders, mutual aid organizers, and frontline community groups historically excluded from institutional philanthropy.

## 7. Key Phrases That Bridge Both Worlds

These phrases honor the philosophy while speaking the language of traditional philanthropy:

- "We practice **trust-based grantmaking with relational accountability** -- combining the agility of unrestricted giving with the rigor of ongoing relationship and structured qualitative reporting."
- "Our grantees are **community-embedded leaders** with deep local knowledge and established trust -- selected through a **rigorous, nomination-based vetting process**, not self-selected through open applications."
- "We organize our grantmaking by **bioregion** because the interconnected environmental, social, and economic challenges facing communities do not respect political boundaries -- and neither should our funding."
- "**Locally-determined resource allocation** ensures that funds respond to conditions as they actually are -- not as they were described in a proposal written months prior."
- "Our **narrative-based outcome reporting** captures the complexity, context, and interconnected nature of community impact in ways that traditional metrics-only reporting cannot."
- "By resourcing **place-based changemakers** working across issue silos, we enable **integrated, systemic impact** -- where a single investment strengthens ecological health, social cohesion, and economic resilience simultaneously."

---

## A Note on Philosophical Integrity

This guide is not about abandoning Kinship Earth's language. "Flow Funding," "emergent needs," and "stories of impact" are meaningful terms that carry the organization's values and lineage. The purpose of this document is to provide a *translation layer* -- so that when writing for audiences steeped in traditional philanthropic frameworks, Kinship Earth can meet them where they are while inviting them into a different way of thinking.

The goal is to open doors with familiar language, then gradually introduce funders to the deeper philosophy. Once a relationship is established, the full vocabulary of Flow Funding, bioregional organizing, and regenerative philanthropy becomes a shared language -- not a barrier.

> Use their language to get in the room. Use your language once you're at the table.
